PearlHacks

In late March, I had the privelege of being invited to mentor teams at PearlHacks, a women-only hackathon at UNC Chapel Hill. This was the very first PearlHacks, and it went really well. I go to a fair number of hackathons because of work, but this one was different in a some pretty important ways:

  1. Creativity. The ideas started flying the instant a new tool was introduced. Typically, I see developers and engineers react to new tools and ideas by trying to find ways to shoot them down. This was the opposite.
  2. Confidence. These engineers and students went straight from the drawing board to the workbench. Again, I’m used to hearing “I’m not a hardware person”/“I’m not a software person”. These developers, in this environment, didn’t spend time telling themselves that parts of their project were opaque or impossible to them. Instead, they hit Google, asked questions, and tried things. And they got it done.
  3. Community. Especially in a competitive environment, it’s not a surprise to see a team belittle another team’s work to bring themselves up. I do this sometimes, even though I think it’s awful. There was absolutely none of this at PearlHacks.

I may have been there to mentor, but I learned a lot. Here’s a quick look at two of the teams I got to hack with over the weekend and what they built:

  • Jenny Xing built a real-world version of Circle Stop, an iOS game by Twenty Percent Games. I was especially floored when I saw it because Circle Stop was built my friend Nolan Filter. It’s a small world! Jenny won Best Hardware Hack and got a kit from Sparkfun for her project.

  • Gloria Li and Fay Wu built a battery-powered, internet-connected silent alarm, and won 2nd place for their project. They definitely deserved the recognition – in 24 hours, they tackled the challenge of building a battery-powered, internet-connected device; learning a new programming language and style (The “Squirrel” language used by Electric Imp is event-driven, which can be really challenging at first); making it compact, and integrating with Twilio. By morning, they had a device that could sent a text or an automated phone call at the touch of a button.

PearlHacks was awesome, and I’m looking forward to using what I learned there to try and help run better events and to be a better engineer. Thanks, PearlHacks!

Let’s Give This a Try

About a month ago, I connected a 24-pixel Neopixel Ring to the Internet and got it to play little animations to show weather conditions.

Right away, I had other ideas for what I could do with it. So I figured an experiment was in order. I’d put up an instructable on how to build it, and see what else people could get it to do. By the end of day one, my friend Aron Steg had already written a package tracker and a clock app (code here) for it.

Since the experiment was going well, I thought I’d put all the different things people programmed their little 24-pixel dials to do with the Internet up on a blog. The only trouble is, I keep meeting people doing even more amazing things, and I already know I’m going to cave and want to share some of it. So here are my field notes.
